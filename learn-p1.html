Revenue Connect

People-Revenue Attribution ~8 min read 3 instruments 🇮🇳 India + 🌍 Global stories
Your best senior hire last year. What did their exit two years ago cost the business — in revenue, not just salary?
Most HR directors can give you the replacement cost. A few can tell you the ramp time. Almost none can tell you the revenue that went with them. The pipeline that cooled. The clients who hesitated. The team that quietly started looking elsewhere. Revenue Connect exists because that number is real — and it's almost always bigger than anyone has admitted.
Beat 1 · The world before
HR has been reporting in the wrong currency.
For decades, the standard HR toolkit has measured attrition as a percentage. Fourteen percent. Twenty-two percent. Nine percent. These numbers are reported, tracked, benchmarked against industry averages, and then largely ignored by the people who make capital decisions — because a percentage tells a CFO nothing useful. A percentage has no rupees in it.
The finance team doesn't report expenses as "22% of budget." Sales doesn't close deals as "a percentage of pipeline." Every other function in the business has learned to speak in absolute numbers because that's the language that commands attention, triggers decisions, and justifies investment. HR got stuck in ratio-land and built its entire identity around it.
The result? HR presents. The board listens politely. Nothing changes. Not because the insights aren't real — but because they were delivered in a language nobody acts on.
🇮🇳 Bengaluru, India Revenue Connect · Story 1
The CHRO who couldn't answer the CFO's question
Ananya had been Chief HR Officer at a 400-person SaaS company in Bengaluru for four years. She was good — genuinely good. Employee NPS was 72. Her L&D programme had won two awards. Attrition was 16%, which she knew was slightly above the industry average of 13% but felt manageable given the growth stage.
In the Q3 board meeting, the CFO asked a single question: "We missed revenue by ₹3.2Cr this quarter. How much of that is a people problem?"
Ananya said she'd get back to him. She never fully did — because she didn't have the number. She knew 23 people had left that quarter. She knew the average replacement cost was about 80% of salary. But the revenue drag — the pipeline that sat unworked, the accounts that didn't get QBRs, the enterprise deal that stalled because the relationship manager had handed in her notice — she'd never quantified any of it.

Beat 2 · The insight
Every exit has three costs — and HR only counts one.
When someone leaves, HR typically calculates one thing: the cost to replace them. Job board fees, recruiter cost, interview time, onboarding. This is the visible cost — the invoice you can point to. But it's the smallest of the three.
The second cost is revenue capacity loss. While the role sits open and while the replacement ramps up — typically 3 to 9 months — your revenue-generating capacity is running below full. A sales rep who was closing ₹40L a quarter doesn't get replaced on day one. There's a gap. That gap has a rupee value.
The third cost is institutional knowledge loss. The relationships, the tribal knowledge, the workarounds, the client context — none of it transfers to the replacement on day one, or even day ninety. For senior roles, this can cost more than the salary itself. Revenue Connect is the framework that adds all three together and converts attrition from a percentage into a financial event.
🇬🇧 London, United Kingdom Revenue Connect · Story 2
The fund manager who changed how his CHRO thought
Marcus had run the people function at a 90-person asset management firm in the City for six years. The MD had always respected him — but that respect had never translated into genuine strategic influence. HR was good at process. It wasn't in the room when real decisions were made.
Then two senior portfolio managers left within three months of each other to join a competitor. Client AUM drifted. One institutional client moved £40M to a competing fund, citing "concerns about team stability." The MD called Marcus in and said something that changed his entire approach: "Can you tell me what those two departures actually cost us? Not HR cost — business cost."
Marcus went away and built the calculation. Replacement cost: £180K. Revenue capacity loss during the 8-month combined ramp period: £620K. Client relationship risk — the AUM that moved: £1.2M in annual fee revenue. Total: £2M from two exits the firm had treated as ordinary attrition.

Beat 3 · The world after
Revenue Connect changes the conversation permanently.
Once an HR professional can say "that exit cost us ₹1.1Cr in revenue, not just ₹18L in replacement cost," the dynamic in every senior meeting shifts. You are no longer reporting on a people metric. You are reporting on a business event with a financial consequence. Those are treated differently by every board member in the room.
The CFO starts asking HR for the number rather than tolerating it. The CEO starts involving people in revenue planning rather than workforce planning. And the HR professional transitions from the person who manages the org chart into the person who manages a material risk to the P&L.
This is not a soft change. It is not about influence or perception. It is about having a number the organisation can act on — and being the person who produces it.
The 3 instruments

How Revenue Connect calculates.

Attrition Cost Percentage
Expresses the full people cost as a revenue drain
ATC%
ATC% = (Total replacement cost of all exits ÷ Annual revenue) × 100
ATC% converts attrition from a headcount rate into a revenue drag rate. When ATC% is 141% — as it was for TechVelocity Inc. — it means that for every ₹100 of revenue, ₹141 was consumed just in replacing departed talent. The business was running backwards.
Worked example: Apex Manufacturing had 4 exits at an average fully-loaded cost of ₹7.5L per replacement. Total replacement cost: ₹30L. Annual revenue: ₹56Cr. ATC% = (30L ÷ 5600L) × 100 = 0.5% — healthy. But when those 4 exits were all in revenue-generating roles, the capacity loss adds another ₹85L. Real ATC%: 2.0%. Still manageable, but very different from 0.5%.
Benchmark: <80% healthy · 80–100% watch zone · >100% critical — you are paying more to replace talent than to generate revenue
Talent Drag Factor
Measures active revenue capacity in transition
TDF
TDF = (Revenue capacity in transition ÷ Total revenue capacity) — expressed as a drag index
TDF asks: of your total revenue-generating headcount, what share is currently not producing at full capacity because they're ramping, backfilling, or managing a gap left by someone else? The lower the TDF index, the more of your headcount is actually at work producing revenue right now rather than compensating for people events.
Worked example: TechVelocity has 50 employees, 30 revenue-generating. 7 exited this year. Average ramp time: 4 months. At any point, roughly 2–3 roles are in transition. TDF = 2.5 ÷ 30 = 0.08 — meaning 8% of your revenue capacity is dragging at any given time. At ₹84Cr annual revenue, that's ₹6.7Cr of annual revenue permanently impaired by transition drag.
Benchmark: TDF below 0.05 = minimal drag · 0.05–0.15 = manageable · above 0.2 = structural revenue impairment
Employee-Sales Ratio
Revenue efficiency of the workforce
ESR
ESR = Annual revenue ÷ (Total headcount × Avg fully-loaded cost per employee) × 100
ESR is the efficiency instrument. It tells you how many rupees of revenue each rupee of people cost generates — and crucially, how that ratio moves after a people event. When ESR drops 10 points after a restructure or a senior departure, you can attribute the efficiency loss directly to that event — and put a rupee figure on it.
Worked example: Mehta & Sons had ESR of 94% before the succession event. After 6 senior exits, ESR fell to 61%. Revenue base: ₹84Cr. The 33-point efficiency drop translates to: 0.33 × ₹84Cr = ₹27.7Cr of revenue efficiency destroyed. That's not a human resources problem. That's a P&L event.
Benchmark: 80%+ threshold for a revenue-efficient organisation · below 60% signals structural people-revenue misalignment
Practitioner's Playbook

Five moves for Monday morning.

You've understood the framework. Here's what a Revenue Fluent practitioner does with it in a real organisation — this week, not this quarter.
1
Run your last 12 months of exits through the ATC% calculation
Pull every exit from the past year. Calculate the real replacement cost (1.5× salary is a reliable starting proxy). Divide by annual revenue. That's your ATC%. If you've never done this, the number will surprise you — and you'll want to have it before the next board meeting.
2
Identify your three highest TDF roles right now
Which revenue-generating roles are currently in transition — open, ramping, or covering for a gap? Calculate the drag each one is causing. Prioritise filling them not by urgency but by revenue drag. The highest-TDF role is your most expensive open position, regardless of seniority.
3
Calculate ESR before and after your last major people event
Pick one significant people event from the past 18 months — a restructure, a senior exit, a team merger. Calculate ESR immediately before and 6 months after. The delta is your attribution evidence. This is the number that proves — or disproves — that the event had a revenue consequence.
4
Build a one-page Revenue at Risk dashboard
Map your current open roles, at-risk talent (anyone you'd score 7+ on flight risk), and roles with below-threshold ESR. This is your revenue risk register — the equivalent of a sales pipeline report, but for people. Present it alongside the P&L, not in the HR deck.
5
Reframe one HR initiative in revenue language before the next leadership meeting
Take one initiative you're already running — a retention programme, a hiring campaign, an L&D investment — and reframe its ROI in revenue terms using the three instruments. "This ₹8L retention programme will prevent 2 exits that would otherwise cost ₹1.4Cr in ATC% and ₹60L in TDF drag." That's a business case, not an HR request.
Key vocabulary
Revenue Connect
The practice of attributing people events — exits, transitions, role changes — to their downstream revenue consequence. The bridge between HR data and financial outcomes.
ATC%
Attrition Cost Percentage. Total replacement cost of all exits in a period, expressed as a percentage of annual revenue. Converts attrition from a headcount metric into a revenue drain metric.
TDF
Talent Drag Factor. The proportion of revenue-generating capacity currently impaired by people in transition. Measures the structural revenue cost of an org that is perpetually replacing itself.
ESR
Employee-Sales Ratio. Revenue generated per unit of people cost. The efficiency instrument — used to measure the before/after impact of any significant people event on revenue productivity.
Revenue at Risk
The total revenue currently exposed to people-related events — open roles, at-risk talent, transitions in progress. The people equivalent of a sales pipeline risk register.
