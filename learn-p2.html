Learning Hub · Pillar 2

People Alpha

Above-Market Returns ~9 min read 4 instruments 🇮🇳 India + 🌍 Global stories
Your company just paid ₹28L to hire a Director of Sales. Six months in, they're performing at 70% of target. Is that good, bad, or indifferent?
Without a benchmark, you literally cannot answer. You don't know if the market average for that hire profile is 60% ramp performance or 85%. You don't know if your L&D investment accelerated their ramp or had no effect. You are managing people decisions the way a fund manager would manage a portfolio without knowing the index return. People Alpha is the index return for every talent decision you make.
Beat 1 · The world before
HR has been measuring activity, not return.
The standard L&D report tells you how many employees completed training. The hiring report tells you cost-per-hire and time-to-fill. The performance report tells you rating distributions. These are activity metrics — they measure what HR did, not what it returned. Measuring activity without measuring return is the equivalent of a fund manager reporting how many trades they made.
People Alpha changes the question from "what did HR do?" to "what did HR return above what the market would have returned if you'd done nothing special?" That is the only question that matters when you're spending real capital on people decisions.
🇮🇳Mumbai, IndiaPeople Alpha · Story 1
The L&D programme that was "working" until someone measured it
Ritu ran L&D at a 300-person financial services firm in Mumbai. Her flagship programme — a six-month leadership accelerator for high-potential managers — had been running for three years. Participation was high. Feedback scores were excellent. The CEO had called it "transformational" in the all-hands.
Then a new CFO joined and asked a question no one had thought to ask: "What's the revenue impact of managers who went through the programme versus those who didn't?"
Ritu pulled the data. The cohort who completed the programme ran teams that produced, on average, 8% higher revenue per head than the control group. The programme cost ₹45L a year. The revenue uplift across the 28 managers who completed it: ₹1.9Cr. That was the number that had been sitting in Ritu's data for three years without anyone calculating it.

"Measuring L&D Alpha didn't just prove the programme worked. It told us precisely where it worked — and where we were burning ₹24L a year on zero return." — Ritu, three months after the CFO's question
Beat 2 · The insight
Four decisions drive People Alpha — and most orgs are negative on at least two.
People Alpha has four dimensions, each corresponding to a different talent capital allocation decision. Hiring Alpha — did you acquire talent that outperforms the market benchmark for that role? Retention Alpha — did you keep the people who compound in value over time? L&D Alpha — did your learning investment generate above-market return? Promotion Alpha — did you promote people who were genuinely ready, or did you create negative alpha by promoting prematurely?
Most organisations, if they measure honestly, find they are positive on 2–3 of these and meaningfully negative on at least one. Promotion Alpha is the most commonly negative dimension — not because companies promote bad people, but because they promote good people before those people are ready, creating under-performance in the new role that quietly erodes return for quarters.
🇩🇪Berlin, GermanyPeople Alpha · Story 2
The promotion that destroyed a team's alpha for eighteen months
Stefan was VP People at a 180-person B2B SaaS company in Berlin. The company was growing fast — 40% year-on-year — and the pressure to promote from within was intense. When the Head of Engineering left, Stefan and the CEO promoted Jana, the most technically brilliant senior engineer, into the role.
Jana was exceptional at what she had been doing. At her previous job, she had been the engineer other engineers called when they were stuck. The promotion felt obvious, deserved, and safe. Her readiness score — had anyone measured it — was 44%. She had never managed more than two people. The new role required leading 22.
Over the next six months, three senior engineers resigned. Two named Jana's management style in their exit interviews. A product launch slipped by 11 weeks. The cost: €380K in replacement hiring, €290K in estimated revenue delay, and the near-loss of a €1.2M enterprise contract that required a stable engineering relationship. Total Promotion Alpha: -€1.87M from one decision.

Beat 3 · The world after
People Alpha converts HR from a cost centre to a portfolio.
When you can demonstrate +22% Hiring Alpha, you are not just saying "we hire well." You are saying "our talent acquisition returns 22 percentage points more than the market benchmark — and here is the revenue impact of that outperformance." That is a return on investment statement. It belongs in the same conversation as marketing ROI and product development return.
The shift in how leadership treats HR decisions is immediate and lasting. Hiring becomes a portfolio allocation question. L&D becomes a yield calculation. Promotions become readiness-gated investments. And the HR professional becomes the person who manages a capital allocation function — not a headcount function.
The 4 alpha types

How People Alpha calculates.

Hiring Alpha
Quality and speed above market benchmark
HA
HA = (Hire quality score vs benchmark) + (Speed-to-productivity premium) − Market baseline
Hiring Alpha measures whether your talent acquisition returns above-market quality and ramp speed, combined. A positive HA means your hires produce more, faster, than the market average for that role profile and compensation level.
Worked example: TechVelocity's new AE hires reach quota attainment in 45 days vs the market average of 72 days. Speed premium: 38%. Quality score (based on 6-month performance): 14% above benchmark. Hiring Alpha: +22%
Benchmark: 0% = market parity · above +15% = strong competitive advantage in talent
Retention Alpha
Value of exits avoided above market rate
RA
RA = (Market exit rate − Your exit rate) × Value per retained person × Tenure compounding factor
Every voluntary exit avoided that the market average would have predicted is a positive return on retention. Senior talent compounds — their institutional knowledge, client relationships, and team influence increase with tenure. Retention Alpha captures that compounding value, not just the replacement cost saved.
Worked example: Market voluntary exit rate for Sterling's profile: 18%. Actual rate: 10%. 4 exits avoided on a 50-person team. Average senior tenure 3.4 years. Retention Alpha: +18% — representing ₹2.1Cr in compounded retained value.
Benchmark: every point below market exit rate is positive alpha · compounds exponentially at senior levels
L&D Alpha
Revenue yield on learning investment
LA
LA = (Skills gap closed × Revenue impact per trained employee) ÷ Programme cost
L&D Alpha links learning investment to revenue output — not hours trained, not completion rates, but the actual skills gap that was closed and its downstream revenue effect. It separates L&D programmes that generate yield from those that merely generate compliance.
Worked example: Ritu's programme closed 78% of identified skills gaps in 28 managers. Revenue per head uplift for completers: 8%. Total uplift: ₹1.9Cr. Programme cost: ₹45L. L&D Alpha: +62% — 4.2× return on investment.
Benchmark: above 50% skills-gap closure = strong programme · L&D Alpha above 2× = high-return investment
Promotion Alpha
Return on promotion — most commonly negative
PA
PA = (Promotion readiness score − 70% threshold) × Role impact weight
Promotion Alpha is the instrument most often ignored — and the one most often negative. A promotion below the readiness threshold is a capital misallocation: it creates under-performance in the new role, team disruption, and the risk of losing a high-performer who was excellent in their previous role but has been set up to fail.
Worked example: Sterling promoted 4 consultants simultaneously. Average readiness score: 62%. Threshold: 70%. Promotion Alpha: -8% — negative alpha that suppressed team utilisation from 78% to 60% over 6 months.
Benchmark: promotions below 70% readiness produce negative alpha · above 85% produces strong compounding return
Practitioner's Playbook

Five moves for Monday morning.

People Alpha only creates value when measured consistently. Here's how to start this week.
1
Establish your industry benchmark for each alpha dimension
Before you can measure alpha, you need a baseline. Pull industry data on time-to-productivity, voluntary exit rates, and L&D ROI for your sector. LinkedIn Talent Insights, SHRM benchmarks, or simply your last 3 years of data as a baseline. This is your index return — everything above it is alpha.
2
Run a promotion readiness audit on your last 8 promotions
Score each promotion retroactively on readiness at the time it was made: management experience, performance in stretch assignments, peer feedback. Then check 6-month post-promotion performance. Find the pattern. Most teams discover a readiness threshold below which promotions reliably underperform.
3
Calculate your L&D Alpha on the three largest programmes this year
For each major training investment, pull: cost, participants, skills-gap measured before and after, and 6-month performance delta. You don't need a controlled experiment — a reasonable before/after comparison is enough to surface the programmes with genuine yield versus those burning budget.
4
Build a retention alpha watchlist for your top 15% earners
Identify the 15% of people whose departure would generate negative Retention Alpha — the ones whose tenure, relationships, and institutional knowledge represent compounding value. These are not necessarily the highest performers by title. They are the highest-value people by tenure impact. Treat retention of this group as a portfolio protection strategy.
5
Present next quarter's people decisions as investment proposals
Frame your next hiring ask, L&D budget request, or retention programme proposal using People Alpha language: "This investment is expected to generate X% alpha above our current baseline." It doesn't need to be precise — directional alpha thinking signals a fundamentally different relationship with the business.
Key vocabulary
People Alpha
The above-market return generated by people decisions — the positive gap between what your talent investments return and what the market benchmark would have returned.
Hiring Alpha (HA)
Above-market return on talent acquisition, measured by quality and speed-to-productivity relative to benchmark.
Retention Alpha (RA)
Value returned by keeping people above market voluntary exit rates — capturing the compounding value of tenure.
L&D Alpha (LA)
Revenue yield on learning investment — skills gap closed multiplied by downstream revenue impact, divided by programme cost.
Promotion Alpha (PA)
Return on promotion decisions — the most commonly negative dimension; driven by whether promotions meet the readiness threshold.
Readiness threshold
The minimum readiness score (typically 70%) below which a promotion reliably generates negative alpha by setting people up to fail.
