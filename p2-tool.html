People Alpha
In finance, alpha is the return you generate above the market benchmark. A fund manager who beats the index produces alpha. One who merely matches it produces none. People Alpha applies this exact concept to your workforce decisions.
Every hire, promotion, retention save, and training investment either beats the market benchmark or falls short of it. People Alpha measures the gap. It turns "we hired well" into "our hiring generated +14% above-market return on talent acquisition."
Two companies can spend identical amounts on people. One generates +22% People Alpha and pulls ahead; the other generates -8% and bleeds advantage. The budget was the same — the alpha was not.
Why it matters
People decisions are capital allocation decisions. When you promote someone, you are betting capital on a return. When you invest in L&D, you expect yield. People Alpha is the only instrument that tells you whether those bets are beating the market or losing to it.
It also exposes the hidden destroyers. Promotion Alpha is the one most often negative — promoting people who aren't ready quietly erodes returns while looking like a reward. People Alpha makes that visible before it compounds.
For a founder or HR leader, this reframes the entire people budget from a cost to be minimised into a portfolio to be optimised for return.
The 4 alpha types
Each instrument isolates a different way this pillar translates people decisions into revenue impact.
Hiring AlphaHA
HA = (Your hire quality + speed-to-productivity) − Market benchmark
Measures whether your hiring beats the market on quality and ramp speed combined. Positive means you acquire talent that produces more, faster, than a typical competitor hiring from the same pool.
Benchmark: 0% = market parity · +15% or more = strong
Retention AlphaRA
RA = Value of voluntary exits avoided × tenure compounding
Captures the above-market return from keeping people who would have left. Retention isn't just cost-avoidance — retained talent compounds in value as tenure deepens institutional knowledge.
Benchmark: each avoided senior exit is worth multiples of salary
L&D AlphaLA
LA = Skills-gap closed × revenue impact per trained employee
The yield on learning investment. It links training directly to revenue capability gained, not hours logged or courses completed — the difference between L&D as a cost centre and L&D as a return engine.
Benchmark: strong programs return 50%+ on skills-gap closure
Promotion AlphaPA
PA = (Promotion readiness − 70% threshold) × impact weight
The most commonly negative instrument. Promoting below the readiness threshold produces negative alpha — it looks like reward but destroys return through under-performance in the new role.
Benchmark: readiness below 70% produces negative alpha
