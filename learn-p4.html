Learning Hub · Pillar 4

HUMANCE

Human Balance Sheet ~10 min read 4 instruments 🇮🇳 India + 🌎 Global stories
A company's most important asset is its people. Why doesn't it appear on the balance sheet?
Because no one has ever developed an accounting standard for it. Goodwill, brand equity, and intellectual property are on the balance sheet — but the trust that keeps clients loyal, the culture that drives discretionary effort, the signal intelligence that flags risk before it becomes a crisis? All of it invisible to the books. Until HUMANCE.
Beat 1 · The world before
Finance can price everything except what matters most.
The accounting profession has spent 150 years developing increasingly sophisticated methods to capture intangible value on the balance sheet. Goodwill was formalised in the late 19th century. Brand valuation became standard in the 1980s. Intellectual property accounting frameworks emerged in the 1990s. In every case, the impetus was the same: a material asset was being destroyed and created without showing up in the financials.
The same problem has existed with human capital for the entire history of business — and has never been solved. The trust a founding team built over a decade. The cultural practices that produce discretionary effort. The informal intelligence networks that surface problems before they become crises. The belonging that keeps exceptional people committed when a competitor offers more. All of it has always been invisible to the books — right up until it's destroyed, at which point the revenue impact is undeniable but untraceable to its cause.
🇮🇳Hyderabad, IndiaHUMANCE · Story 1
The family business that couldn't see its own net worth
The Mehta family had built a 50-person components business over three decades. The financials looked straightforward: ₹84Cr in revenue, ₹9Cr in tangible assets, ₹48Cr in payroll and operating costs. On paper, a healthy business. What didn't appear anywhere on those financials was the ₹12.1Cr in HUMANCE assets that underpinned every rupee of that revenue.
The Trust Capital — Rajesh Mehta's 30-year relationships with the three largest clients, which collectively represented 62% of revenue. The Signal Intelligence — the informal network of senior employees who had navigated every supply chain crisis of the past decade and knew things about the business that weren't written anywhere. The Belonging Premium — a culture of radical ownership where employees routinely went beyond their job descriptions because they felt genuinely part of something.
When Rajesh announced his retirement and his son Arjun took over, none of this was measured, priced, or protected. It was assumed it would transfer with the title. It did not. Six senior employees left within 18 months — not because of anything Arjun did wrong, but because the trust they'd had in Rajesh was personal and non-transferable. Revenue fell 31% before anyone understood why.

Beat 2 · The insight
HUMANCE prices what finance has always left blank.
HUMANCE is not a metaphor. It is a measurement framework with four discrete asset classes, each with a calculable value tied to observable business metrics. Trust Capital is priced through knowledge concentration risk and succession readiness — what is the cost when key relationships prove non-transferable? Culture Yield is priced through the efficiency gap before and after a culture-disrupting event. Signal Intelligence is priced through the value of early-warning capacity that exits represent. Belonging Premium is priced through the discretionary effort coefficient — the revenue that occurs only because people want to, not because they must.
The total HUMANCE figure is not a guess or a benchmark. It is a calculated value grounded in the same metrics that flow through the other four pillars — ESR, exit data, revenue at risk. Revenue tells you what you earned. HUMANCE tells you what made earning possible.
🇺🇸San Francisco, USAHUMANCE · Story 2
The acquisition that paid $40M for a company and got $12M of it
A Bay Area enterprise software company acquired a 60-person product team in 2019 for $40M. The deal was justified by the product roadmap, the client base, and the team — particularly three senior architects whose relationships with the four largest enterprise clients were described as "irreplaceable" in the acquisition memo.
Standard due diligence was done. Technical debt was assessed. Client contracts were reviewed. HUMANCE was never calculated. The Trust Capital embedded in those three senior architects — the relationship depth built over years of enterprise delivery — was valued at precisely $0 in the deal structure.
Within 14 months, all three architects had left. The earn-out structure had been tied to product milestones, not retention. Without specific financial incentives to stay, and without the culture they'd built being preserved through the integration, they left for a startup. Two of the four enterprise clients followed them. Revenue from those accounts: $8.4M annually.
Post-acquisition review found that $12M of the $40M valuation had been attributable to HUMANCE assets that evaporated in the integration. The acquirer paid full price for a partial business.

Beat 3 · The world after
HUMANCE makes the invisible financially legible.
When HUMANCE is calculated and tracked, three things change permanently. First, people decisions are made with full cost visibility — the HUMANCE consequences of a succession event, an acquisition integration, or a culture change are priced before the decision, not discovered after. Second, the balance sheet argument for people investment becomes defensible — "this retention programme costs ₹8L and protects ₹3.2Cr in Trust Capital" is a CFO-grade investment case. Third, leadership can answer the question they've never been able to answer: what is this company really worth if you strip out its human assets?
For founders, CHROs, and HR professionals who have always known that their people are their most valuable asset but have never been able to prove it numerically — HUMANCE is the proof.
The instruments

The 4 HUMANCE asset classes.

Trust Capital
Relationship depth and knowledge concentration risk
TC
TC = f(IRD score, RBC score) × revenue at risk coefficient
Trust Capital prices the value embedded in trusted relationships and the risk when knowledge is concentrated in too few people. The IRD (Institutional Relationship Depth) score measures how deeply embedded key relationships are — and how transferable they are. The RBC (Relationship Bridge Coverage) score measures succession readiness for those relationships.
Worked example: Mehta & Sons: IRD 18% (dangerously concentrated), RBC 20% (minimal succession coverage), revenue at risk ₹84Cr. Trust Capital destroyed: ₹2.8Cr — the value of relationships that proved non-transferable in the succession.
Driven by: IRD concentration + RBC readiness + revenue exposure of key relationships
Culture Yield
Revenue efficiency produced by cultural practices
CY
CY = (ESR pre-event − ESR post-event) × annual revenue base
Culture Yield prices the revenue-producing efficiency of cultural practices — and what evaporates when a culture-disrupting event occurs. It links culture directly to the Employee-Sales Ratio: when culture degrades, ESR drops, and the delta is the Culture Yield destroyed.
Worked example: Mehta & Sons: ESR 94% before succession, 61% after. Revenue base ₹84Cr. ESR drop: 33 points. Culture Yield destroyed: ₹27.7Cr — the efficiency loss from a culture that took 30 years to build and 18 months to partially dismantle.
Driven by: the ESR gap before and after a culture-disrupting people event
Signal Intelligence
Early-warning capacity and institutional knowledge value
SI
SI = f(revenue at risk, exit cascade severity, knowledge concentration)
Signal Intelligence prices the value of the informal early-warning network embedded in experienced people — the institutional knowledge that surfaces supply chain problems before they become crises, client dissatisfaction before it becomes churn, team dynamics before they become attrition. When that network exits, the signal disappears.
Worked example: When Mehta's 6 senior employees left, the company lost its informal supply chain intelligence network. Within 6 months, a sourcing problem that would previously have been flagged 8 weeks early caused a production delay worth ₹3.7Cr. Signal Intelligence value: ₹3.7Cr.
Driven by: exit volume × revenue at risk × institutional knowledge depth score
Belonging Premium
Discretionary effort value above employment contract obligations
BP
BP = exits × discretionary-effort coefficient × revenue base per FTE
Belonging Premium prices the revenue generated by people doing more than their job description requires — the call made after hours, the problem solved proactively, the client relationship deepened beyond scope. This discretionary effort is only possible when belonging is genuine. When belonging erodes, performance falls to the floor of the contract.
Worked example: Mehta & Sons: culture of radical ownership meant employees routinely delivered 20–30% above contracted output. Post-succession, discretionary effort fell to near-zero. Revenue impact of lost Belonging Premium: ₹4.5Cr annually.
Driven by: cultural belonging depth × discretionary effort coefficient × revenue per FTE
Practitioner's Playbook

Five moves for Monday morning.

HUMANCE is the hardest pillar to measure — and the most important to protect. Here's how to start making it visible in your organisation.
1
Map your knowledge concentration risk this week
Ask one question: if three specific people left tomorrow, what revenue would be at risk? Not headcount — specific people. Name them. Identify the client relationships, institutional knowledge, and team chemistry that sits with them. That map is your Trust Capital risk register.
2
Calculate your Culture Yield before and after your last major event
Take a significant people event from the past 2 years — a leadership change, a restructure, a merger. Calculate ESR before and 6-12 months after. Multiply the delta by annual revenue. That's how much Culture Yield the event cost. This number will surprise most leaders.
3
Build a succession coverage score for your top 10 roles
For each of your 10 most revenue-critical roles, score succession readiness 0-100. How ready is the next person? The weighted average of these scores is your RBC. Below 40% means Trust Capital is dangerously concentrated. This is your acquisition risk, your succession risk, and your retention investment priority.
4
Identify your Signal Intelligence sources
Which people in your organisation consistently surface problems before they escalate? These are your signal intelligence nodes. They are rarely the most senior or highest-paid. Map them. They carry HUMANCE value far above their compensation — and their exit destroys it without appearing in any cost centre.
5
Present HUMANCE as a balance sheet item at the next senior meeting
Run a rough HUMANCE calculation for your organisation — even directional numbers work. Present the four asset classes with estimated values. The purpose is not precision; it is to make the conversation happen. The question "what are our human assets worth?" needs to be in the room.
Key vocabulary
HUMANCE
A coined currency representing the total value of an organisation's four human intangible asset classes: Trust Capital, Culture Yield, Signal Intelligence, and Belonging Premium.
Trust Capital (TC)
The value embedded in trusted relationships and institutional knowledge depth — priced through knowledge concentration risk and succession readiness.
Culture Yield (CY)
The revenue efficiency produced by cultural practices — measured through the ESR gap before and after a culture-disrupting event.
Signal Intelligence (SI)
The value of the informal early-warning intelligence network embedded in experienced people — quantified through exit cascade severity and institutional knowledge depth.
Belonging Premium (BP)
The revenue generated through discretionary effort above employment contract obligations — only possible when belonging is genuine.
IRD
Institutional Relationship Depth. A 0-100 score measuring how deeply embedded and concentrated key business relationships are.
RBC
Relationship Bridge Coverage. A 0-100 score measuring succession readiness for key relationships — the inverse of Trust Capital concentration risk.
