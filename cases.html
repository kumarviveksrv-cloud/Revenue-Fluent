The CEO calls the VP HR into her office. She wants an explanation. The VP Sales joined 4 months ago. Two of the senior Account Executives left in the same period. The pipeline has a gap. The CEO's question: "Is this a people problem or a market problem?"

The intervention: what should have happened
🔒
Knowing what Revenue Connect reveals, let's rewind. What would a Revenue Fluent HR professional have done differently — and at what stage?
Stage 1: At VP hire. A Revenue Fluent practitioner would have calculated the HUMANCE exposure before the hire: how much Trust Capital was embedded in the existing AE team's relationship with their previous manager? IRD score: high. RBC (Relationship Bridge Coverage): low — no succession plan for the team leadership relationship. This alone flags the hire as high-HUMANCE-risk and triggers a 90-day trust onboarding protocol.
Stage 2: At the first flight-risk signal. The ESR began dropping in Month 2 after the VP joined — from 85% to 72%. A Revenue Fluent practitioner tracks ESR monthly and flags any drop above 8 points in a single period as a Culture Yield erosion signal. The intervention window was there. It wasn't used.

Case Study 2

The Promotions That Cost £2.4M

Sterling Consulting promoted four senior consultants simultaneously. Billable utilisation crashed. Revenue missed. Work through the People Alpha diagnosis.

1
The situation: utilisation has collapsed
▼
Sterling Consulting Group is a 50-person management consulting firm in London. Their revenue model is simple: billable hours multiplied by day rate. For four years, utilisation had sat at a stable 78%. Then Q3 arrived and it was 60%.
In the same quarter, four senior consultants had been promoted to Associate Director. The promotions were celebrated. The firm's Managing Partner described them as "overdue recognition of outstanding performers." Nobody connected the promotions to the utilisation drop.

All four promotions were made at an average readiness score of 62% — below the 70% threshold. Promotion Alpha: -8%. The revenue consequence of 4 under-ready promotions managing teams for the first time: £2.4M in annual billing capacity destroyed.
The Managing Partner says the promotions were earned. Is he wrong?
Correct: right people, wrong timing — readiness was at 62%, below the 70% threshold. People Alpha doesn't say don't promote these people. It says don't promote them before they're ready. 8 percentage points below threshold generated -8% Promotion Alpha and destroyed £2.4M in annual revenue. The same promotions 2 quarters later, with preparation, would have generated positive alpha.
2
The recovery: restoring People Alpha
🔒
The question is now: what does a Revenue Fluent practitioner do to restore People Alpha after four simultaneous under-ready promotions?
The four newly promoted ADs aren't failing — they're struggling in specific dimensions: client management at senior level (all four), commercial negotiation (three of four), and team performance management (two of four). These are identifiable skills gaps, not character flaws.
A targeted 3-month accelerated readiness programme — focused on the specific gaps — would cost approximately £60K. The revenue recovery from restoring utilisation from 60% to 72% (not all the way to 78% — realistic for mid-year) on a £12M revenue base: +£1.44M. L&D Alpha on this investment: 24×.
What's the correct intervention proposal to bring to the Managing Partner?
Correct: targeted L&D Alpha investment framed as acceleration, not remediation. Rolling back promotions destroys morale and trust. External hires are expensive and slow. The £60K programme addresses the specific gaps identified by the readiness analysis and is presented to the MP as a business case with a 24× return — which it is. The People Alpha lens converts an embarrassing situation into a smart investment.

The HUMANCE Implosion

The hardest case in the platform. A succession event destroys ₹12.1Cr in HUMANCE across four asset classes. Work through the diagnosis and build the recovery People P&L.

1
The situation: revenue fell 31% and nobody knows why
▼
Mehta & Sons Enterprises is a 50-person industrial components business in Hyderabad, operating for 35 years. Annual revenue: ₹84Cr. Rajesh Mehta, the founder, retired 18 months ago. His son Arjun took over. Revenue is now ₹58Cr — a 31% decline.
The board is asking what happened. There is no market explanation — the sector has grown 4% in the same period. Three competitors have grown. Mehta & Sons is the only company in their peer group to have declined. And nobody has connected the revenue drop to the succession event.

Total: ₹12.1Cr — 14.4% of annual revenue — destroyed in 18 months.
The board asks: is the revenue decline recoverable? What's the honest answer?
Correct: partially recoverable, 3–5 year horizon. This is the hardest truth HUMANCE delivers. Trust Capital is relationship-based — it is rebuilt through Arjun demonstrating sustained reliability over time, not through a programme or a spend. Signal Intelligence rebuilds as new senior people develop tenure. The honest answer to the board is: "We have a 3–5 year recovery road, not a 1-year fix. Here's what it looks like and what it costs to accelerate."
2
The recovery People P&L: what should have been done
🔒
The question is no longer "what happened?" — that's answered. The question is: what would a Revenue Fluent practitioner have done before the succession that would have protected the HUMANCE?
Before the handover: A HUMANCE audit would have flagged IRD of 18% and RBC of 20% as critical — Rajesh's relationships were dangerously concentrated with no succession coverage. The intervention: a structured 24-month succession programme where Rajesh and Arjun jointly managed key client relationships, gradually transferring trust before the handover.
The cost of a proper succession programme: ₹45L over 24 months — consulting support, structured client introductions, leadership coaching. The cost of not doing it: ₹12.1Cr in HUMANCE destruction and ₹26Cr in annual revenue loss.
