The 3 Team Configurations

Every team in every company on earth falls into one of three configurations. Not by industry. Not by size. By the structural relationship between roles, spans of control, and revenue accountability. Click each to explore.

Configuration A
High-Autonomy Revenue Pod
Team size 5-8 people
Manager ratio 1:6
Decision layers 2
+34% revenue
vs company average per team
A small, autonomous pod with a builder-archetype manager, low meeting load, direct access to customers, and clear revenue accountability per person. Every member knows exactly what revenue they own. Speed of decision: hours, not days. This is the highest-performing configuration in data-driven and sales organisations.
Configuration B
Matrix Coordination Team
Team size 12-20 people
Manager ratio 1:10
Decision layers 3-4
+8% revenue
vs company average per team
A larger team with dotted-line reporting, shared ownership across functions, and coordination-heavy processes. Revenue accountability is diffused – everyone owns a piece but nobody owns the whole. Decision speed: days to weeks. Common in enterprises. Generates modest returns above average but rarely outperforms Configuration A.
Configuration C
Top-Heavy Hierarchy
Team size 15-30 people
Manager ratio 1:4
Decision layers 5+
-19% revenue
vs company average per team
A management-heavy structure where coordination costs exceed output value. Too many layers between customer and decision-maker. Revenue accountability is unclear. Senior people spend time in meetings rather than generating output. Decision speed: weeks to months. Common in over-promoted orgs – exactly what happened at Sterling Consulting and Apex Manufacturing.
The Key Insight

When you deviate from your highest-performing configuration – through a restructure, a wave of promotions, a merger, or a headcount freeze – you don't just change the org chart. You change the revenue output. Predictably. Measurably.

Every Company Has a Revenue DNA

Revenue DNA is the unique combination of team structures, leadership archetypes, and org design patterns that produce revenue in YOUR company. It's not the same as competitors. It's not in any management textbook. It's yours.

What Revenue DNA reveals
→ Which team sizes generate the most output per FTE in YOUR org
→ Which leadership archetypes consistently outperform
→ Which spans of control create velocity vs. bottlenecks
→ Which structural changes predictably reduce output
What destroys Revenue DNA
→ Restructures that break high-performing team configurations
→ Promotions that move revenue generators into cost roles
→ Headcount freezes in the wrong layers
→ Merging high-performing pods into larger, slower teams
The Apex Manufacturing Case

Apex's Revenue DNA was simple: small autonomous operator teams (5-7 people) with one experienced supervisor per team. This generated 112% of industry benchmark output per line.

Two restructures broke this DNA. They went from Configuration A to Configuration C. Output dropped 22%. The ops director blamed equipment. HR had the real answer: the org chart changed. Revenue changed with it.

HR's New Question

The CEO asks: "Should we restructure the production team?" The CFO asks: "What will this do to our cost base?" The new Revenue Fluent CHRO asks the question nobody else thinks to ask.

"Does this restructure strengthen or weaken our Revenue DNA?"

The CEO can't answer that. The CFO can't answer it. Only the CHRO can – because only the CHRO has the people-decision-to-revenue data.

Old CHRO question
"How will this affect morale and engagement?"
Board response: polite nod, no action
Revenue Fluent CHRO question
"This moves us from Config A to Config C. Based on our Revenue DNA, that's a projected -19% output. Here's the revenue cost."
Board response: restructure paused, alternatives explored
